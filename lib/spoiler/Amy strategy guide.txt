This is a strategy guide for ToME-SX written by AmyBSOD. It's supposed to give hints on how to play this variant, based on my own experiences with the game, and will hopefully be useful for newbies although some of it will be rather advanced stuff for the late game which a newbie won't reach anyway. Keep in mind that I'm no Lord Dimwit, so I'm not all that skilled at the game; in fact, I'd consider myself a noob even though I've managed to stumble my way to a few ascensions (one with the warrior and one with a rogue) at the time of writing. Incidentally, I started writing this document at March 27 of 2026.

If your text editor doesn't have line wrap, this document will probably look weird, so I *highly recommend* to turn line wrap on (use a text editor that has this feature, WordPad for example, which is what I used to write it) :-P

*** Other spoiler documents ***

Please refer to "amycontrols.txt" to learn the various important commands of this game, or refer to the in-game help system. This document assumes that you at least know how to walk around, equip items etc. If not, read the aforementioned documentation.

For a map of the game world, refer to tomemapNEW.png please. The default starting location is the town of Bree. Towns are white * (asterisks) on the map while dungeons are generally purple > (greater-than symbol, denoting staircases).

The various towns, dungeons and wilderness areas encountered in the game world are detailed in "towns and dungeons.txt". While you can use the < key in a town or wilderness to go to world map mode and explore, you'd better stay away from high-level areas unless your character is strong enough to handle them; in particular, the ocean is incredibly deadly due to high-level ambushes, but even shore or forest ambushes can be surprisingly dangerous. Mountains and volcanoes also tend to have highly dangerous ambushes. Quaffing a potion of repelling will temporarily reduce your risk of being ambushed by a factor 10, which can make high-level terrain much safer but be aware that there's still a small remaining chance of being ambushed.
If you do get ambushed in the wilderness, you have to reach the edge of the map to escape. It's also possible to escape an ambush by reading a scroll of recall and waiting for it to kick in, or if there's a staircase (quest, dungeon or otherwise), taking it will stop the ambush too. Finally, if you're ambushed in a town, going to the mayor and getting a new quest may stop the ambush too, but only if doing so causes the town to be regenerated.

*** Character creation ***

The first thing you do when starting a new game is to create a character. There's incredibly many possible choices so I'll try to give a brief overview for beginners here.

Gender is just cosmetic, so pick whatever you want here.

Race mostly determines your starting stats, some of these also have special abilities; human is the default and probably not a bad choice for beginners. The various elves mostly have good mental stats, dwarves have good physical stats, trolls and ogres are super strong but dumb, rohanknight has a very powerful speed bonus but sucky stealth, thunderlord is very strong and gains various resistances but also sucks at stealth, maia has super stats all across the board but aggravates monsters (BAD), and deathmold can't walk normally but has special abilities and superb stealth.
For a beginner, I'd recommend either human, or especially for a fighter, something like dwarf or troll. Elf may be good for mage type classes but it makes their anemic HP totals even worse. The rohanknight is probably also a rather solid choice since speed is the god stat of this game and makes you pretty much better at everything.

Race modifiers can further tweak your character, these can give useful bonuses (e.g. resistances) but usually with major drawbacks. If you're new at the game, I'd recommend using Classical, i.e. the default selection. LostSoul in particular is a super difficult mode that causes you to start on dungeon level 98 with no way to access the town; I can't for the life of me figure out how the hell you're supposed to survive in that mode so unless you're Lord Dimwit or a similar pro player, don't pick this one ;)

Classes are divided into sub-classes of the six "main" classes. Each main class has a variety of actual classes to pick from but they're mostly similar to each other so I'll just explain the main classes here, feel free to experiment with the various sub-classes to see what they do.
Warrior has the best HP multiplier and is great for melee combat. Just use a powerful weapon and bash away. The sub-classes mostly determine which particular type of weapon you're really good at. Downside is that warriors usually have sucky saving throws and not much else besides melee. My second ascension was with the Haftedmaster. IMHO one of the strongest classes.
Archer is mostly good at ranged combat, with the sub-class determining which type of ranged weapon you're good at. They're not all that good at melee, and ammo management can be annoying. But if they max out their ranged skills, they can deal great damage per shot.
Rogue is generally a melee fighter, similar to the Warrior, but most of the time they need to specialize in daggers since they're the only weapon type that benefits from the Critical-Hits skill which most sub-classes of rogue will have. Some types of rogue also have a few spell skills available, but most importantly they have great multipliers in the sneakiness skills, especially Stealth which allows them to move around without waking up all that many monsters. Maxxing out Stealth can help tremendously. My first ascension was with the Assassin, a sub-class tailored towards melee combat.
Mage is the spellcaster role, they have lots of spell schools but super sucky melee. They basically need to cast spells to kill things, so you'll need to pump points into spell schools that have damage-dealing spells, and also max the hell out of the Magic skill to improve your max mana. You can also learn various support spells, like teleportation or detection, which will also help you. However, they have the lowest HP multiplier of all the classes and are therefore rather squishy. I generally don't get far if I'm playing one of these.
Priest is a melee/caster hybrid, their melee isn't very good but at least it's better than the Mage. Their spells mostly come from the Prayer skill, which can grant various spell schools depending on the god you worship, but generally at lower spell levels than the Mage so the spells will be less effective. I often have a hard time with this class since both their melee and spellcasting tends to fall behind the curve at some point.
Loremaster is the most diverse class, most of them have the bare-handed combat skill which allows them to fight without weapons but that one tends to fall behind the curve late into the game. They can also use weapons but are quite a bit less skilled at weapon combat compared to the Warrior or Rogue. Most importantly, though, the sub-class of your Loremaster will determine which additional skills you can use: there's stuff like Mimicry, Symbiosis, Possession or Summoning, which may give you an edge if you pump up the respective skills.

Generally, I'd recommend the warrior or rogue if you're new to the game as they're IMHO the easiest to play. Loremaster (pursuing the bare-handed combat skill) can also be good during the early game since your melee damage output will scale directly with your skill level without needing to find a good weapon. Figuring out how to play a priest or mage is IMHO something you should only do when you're more experienced at the game, and the archer's tendency to run out of ammo makes it less ideal as a beginner's role too.

The god worshipped by your character determines which spell schools you can use via the Prayer skill, and can give you various benefits if you up that god's piety but some gods will also give drawbacks if your piety becomes too negative. If you don't want to worry about them, you can always pick "No God" (unless you pick a priest type who has to worship a god), but believing in a god can also give significant boosts to your character if you know what you're doing. I'll give an outline of what the various gods do later in this document, but for a beginner I'll say this:
Don't worship AmyBSOD or Ingeborg; those give hefty drawbacks when your piety becomes negative, and during the early game it's almost impossible to get your piety back up to positive values.
Don't worship Eru Iluvatar, Nienna or Este; those give insurmountable penalties when wielding any edged weapon, greatly limiting the equipment choices available to you.
The other gods mostly don't have drawbacks; Manwe Sulimo may reduce your speed if your piety becomes too negative but only when you're praying, so just don't pray when worshipping him (or at the very least, don't pray if your piety is too negative) and you'll be fine.

Now the game will ask you whether you want to change the options. These are birth options, meaning you can *only* set them at game start; changing them during an ongoing game is not possible. I'll try to explain what they do and which settings I'd recommend:
Maximise stats - I generally have this set to "yes", it changes the maximum possible values for your character's stats in some way and IIRC turning it off means all chars will max out at the same values, greatly reducing role/race distinction.
Level scaling - in ToME-SX this defaults to "yes", and means that monsters will scale in level depending on the current area's level. Turning this off is basically easy mode because then monsters generally won't scale in this way unless you're in an area specifically designed to have level scaling. I generally always have this option enabled.
Preserve artifacts - you really should leave this on, because if you turn it off, it means that once an artifact generates on a level you *must* pick it up or it's lost forever. And who has the patience to explore every single level just to make sure you don't lose a useful artifact forever??? So, yeah, you shouldn't turn this option off, ever.
Specify 'minimal' stats - dunno what exactly this does, I think it's about the initial stat roll when creating a character? It defaults to "yes", apparently, and I've never experimented with it so I have no idea what happens if you turn it off.
Generate character using a point system - I think this also deals with setting your character's starting stats? It defaults to "no", and I don't remember ever trying out what happens if you turn it on.
Generate persistent dungeons - DO NOT TURN THIS ON! The game says this is an "experimental" option; if you come from NetHack (like I did when I first discovered ToME), you may think you'll want to play with this enabled, but the truth is: you don't want this to be enabled. It means that dungeon levels are semi-persistent but you still need to explore them every time, and at the same time it *disables the random quests in the dungeon*. Since those quests give skill boosts that cannot be gained in any other way, playing with persistent dungeons means you miss out on these quests completely and will therefore end up with a much weaker character in the end. So, be wise and leave this option at the "no" setting it starts on.
Always generate very unusual rooms - "Ironman mode", this means that the dungeon levels will be filled with greater vaults and stuff. This massively changes the way the game will play, you can encounter highly leveled monsters even on dungeon level 1 as a result but may also find great loot. Unless you're a fairly experienced player, you probably shouldn't use this mode.
Allow notes to be written to a file - I think this allows you to use an in-game notepad? I've never used this feature but feel free to experiment ;)
Automatically note important events - I think this causes certain in-game events to automatically be logged to said in-game notepad?
Fast autoroller - no idea what that does?!
Hard mode - Some players have complained that I supposedly made the game easier compared to vanilla ToME 2.3.5. Well, I dare those clones of Lord Dimwit (which is probably what they are, because everyone else would feel that ToME-SX is pretty damn challenging :P) to play the game with this mode enabled and see if they still find it too easy! :D It significantly reduces your maximum HP and sanity, to-hit and to-damage bonuses as well as speed, making the game much harder. Of course, if you're a beginner you should definitely keep this option turned off.
Masochist mode - Another hard mode specifically meant for those players who think the game is too easy. It reverts some of ToME-SX's changes to vanilla ToME 2.3.5 behavior, such as monster spell and breath damage calculations. Generally, a lot of things become more deadly if you turn this option on. And yes, you can theoretically turn both this and hard mode on for extra high difficulty, but again, unless you're deliberately looking for a challenge, keep this option turned off.
Allow use of some 'joke' monsters - You can't turn this off. It only appears in the menu because I can't be bothered to figure out how to remove it from the menu. Even if you do attempt to turn it off, it'll automatically turn itself back on once you start your character because you're not supposed to turn joke monsters off. Sorry :-P
Always make small levels - This causes the dungeon generator to, well, make smaller levels. IMHO that takes away some of the flavor from the game because certain dungeons are meant to have big levels. I never turn this option on but I guess it's there if you think exploring large levels takes too long?
You can receive fates, good or bad - This means that every once in a while, you can receive a fate to encounter something specific on a given dungeon level. Some of these can be highly useful (e.g. finding an artifact) while others can be super dangerous (e.g. encountering a powerful boss enemy). I never turn this option off because IMHO fates are an integral part of the game but I guess it's possible to disable them if you somehow find them annoying?

Now the game will ask you for the "number of quests" (unless you happened to set some terribly wrong options, in which case you should start over and turn those pesky persistent dungeon levels off). As a rule of thumb you should ALWAYS go with the default number of 980, because then you'll get a random quest on every single dungeon level. As I said before, completing these quests will give permanent bonuses to your skills and taking less quests just means you'll receive less such bonuses, thereby permanently reducing the maximum power level your character can achieve. Really, trust me on this one, you'll want all the skill bonuses you can get because without them, your character will only be able to max out maybe 2-3 skills total and that's not nearly enough to have a chance later on. Unless you're Lord Dimwit, in which case you'll probably still manage to own everything even when taking 0 quests, but even he would probably want to take those quests to boost his character's power. So just press Enter and you'll have 980 quests.

Now you can roll your stats. There's probably a myriad of ways to do this, by e.g. specifying minimum values for some stats or using the point-based system or whatever, but I just let the game roll random values and go with whatever I get. STR and DEX are useful for melee characters and high enough values will increase the amount of blows you get with your weapon, INT is good for spellcasters and WIS can be good for priests, and CON is useful for everyone because CON means hit points. CHA is generally the least useful stat since it mostly just affects vendor prices.

Finally, the game will let you edit your character's name and flavor text, and then you're in the game at last! You're the @ symbol, and you're in the town of Bree where your adventure begins. There's various things you can do, and also various ways to get yourself killed. The following guide should give some general hints on how to get started.

*** Game start ***

Maybe you started with a weapon or armor piece; these don't start out equipped, so you should probably equip them. Then, generally I always want a brass lantern and pick with every char; you can buy them at the general store. Also empty bottles, these cannot be bought directly but you can buy a couple dozen pints of ale and "eat" them (really you're supposedly drinking them but it's done with the eat command, so there :-P) and then you'll have bottles which you can fill when you come upon fountains in the dungeon. You can also buy a weapon or some armor if you need it, and generally I recommend always having a few teleport scrolls and healing potions so buy some of them too. Later on, when you've gone down to dungeon level 4 or so, you always want to have some recall scrolls on your person, and eventually you also need trap detection (e.g. via scrolls) but you usually don't need that right away. Spellcaster roles will want to check out the bookstore periodically in the hopes of finding good spellbooks; fireproof ones in particular are valuable since non-proofed ones can be destroyed by the rampant item destruction. And the magic shop has not only some low-level rings and amulets that grant basic resistances and stuff, but also the very useful wand of noxious cloud that can be a strong early-game crowd control item. Staves of teleportation are also sold here: you'll thank the gods for their existence if/when something chain-confuses/blinds you making your scrolls not work.

Every town has a home, but you always need to complete a quest first. Once you enter, there's no way to leave until you completed the house quest, so make sure you're ready. The Bree house has rogues and a level 10 bandit, which are annoying but at least they often teleport away when they attack (even if they fail a steal) allowing you to rest if you've taken damage; the novice warriors in that quest can be much more threatening since those will aggressively chase you and not back down. Powerful melee chars may be able to complete the quest right away, and you'll also generally level up to 5-6 in there so if you can off the rogues you might then use your skill points to become powerful enough for the warriors. Anyway, having a home is important so you can save all the loot you find so you should definitely try to complete the thieves quest soon.

The town itself spawns various "town monsters", which are mostly stuff like tourists but also occasionally dangerous stuff like mean-looking mercenaries. These monsters can be killed for the occasional drop of a bit of money or crappy items, and they can also leave corpses which you can eat or hack up; you can sell any meat you don't need to the general store for a few zorkmids. So if you're out of funds and need basic supplies, you can farm the town for a while to gain a bit of money. However, if you need to level up, e.g. due to not being ready for the thieves quest yet, you might have to go to the wilderness. Beware: hitting <, or leaving the town to one of the sides, has a chance of you being ambushed. If that happens, you need to reach the edge of the map to stop the ambush or otherwise leave the area; recall or teleport level works, but you probably won't have that available when you just start out. Anyway, if you're level 1 don't leave the town to the west as the overworld square directly west of Bree has a special quest with vicious dogs who will maul you good, and also don't leave to the south since that'll lead to the swamp where stuff like nagas or maybe even hydras will also kill you very quickly. Grass areas to the east or north are good for killing low-level stuff like ants or jackals. Definitely don't make the mistake of walking to a mountain tile in local mode (you can't do that in world map mode unless you have a climbing set, which will very probably not be the case early on) or you'll get something like storm giants or mature green dragons and die pretty much instantly :-P

Completed the Thieves Quest? Great, you now have a home; you can talk to the mayor now for some pep talk, and doing so at this point will even stop an ambush if there's one going on, but beware that talking to him decides what your next Bree quest will be. If your combat skill is higher than your magic skill, talk to him and you have a 95% chance of getting the trolls quest. Otherwise, *hold off*. Even if you're a mage, and even if you had to increase your magic skill to 10 just to survive. Hold off on that quest for as long as it takes until you can get some good combat skill, probably with the help of Fumblefingers (more on that later), and *do not talk to the mayor* unless combat is very definitely at least one level above magic. Because if magic is higher than combat at the time of talking to the mayor, you get the wights quest instead which is no fun and has a totally garbage reward. The trolls quest at least gives you a moderately powerful artifact sword. Then again, even if you do get the trolls quest, you did read that part about the difficulty level being something like 30, right? Take that information seriously. As in, don't attempt to actually do that quest unless your char is strong enough to reliably handle monsters on dungeon level 30.

The game's first dungeon, the Barrow-Downs, are accessed in the western half of Bree. This dungeon goes from level 1 to 10 and will keep you busy for a while. You can also venture a bit to the east of Bree on the overworld map instead and go to Barad-Dur, another dungeon that starts out at level 1, or if you dare to cross the river (make sure you have a bunch of teleport scrolls that you can read to reach the edge in case you get ambushed!), a bit to the northeast of Barad-Dur you find the Wild Mine which is yet another level 1 dungeon. But careful: the boss on level 10 of Wild Mine can kill you quickly if you don't know what you're doing.

*** God quests ***

If you're worshipping a god, every once in a while you'll get a quest upon leveling up. These quests task you to find a forgotten temple somewhere on the world map, and within that temple, find and pick up a relic for some free boosts to your prayer skill. Now there's several caveats with these quests:
- The temple can spawn in a dangerous wilderness area, so you might not be strong enough to survive the trip there until you're a lot stronger. Potions of repelling can greatly reduce, but not nullify the risk of being ambushed; if the quest is in the ocean or something, and your char is only level 10 or so, *stay away*! Also, if the temple is far away from Bree, make sure you bring enough food and light; you don't want to run out in the middle of nowhere and die with no way of resupplying.
- Leaving the level that has the relic will cause the quest to fail; in vanilla this used to make the entire relic quest line fail permanently, but here in SX you'll instead lose a lot of piety and stats, and can then try again. Still, such failures are to be avoided at all costs. Every temple level has a 1 in 5 chance of having the relic so in theory you have to search everywhere; potions of sense objects or enlightenment will map out all the items on a level, so the easiest way is to bring a couple of those and quaff one whenever you enter a dungeon level in the temple, then scan the map (without actually moving your character, of course) to see whether the relic is there. If it is, dash to it and pick it up but don't forget to detect traps, you don't want to hit a trapdoor or level teleporter before you have the relic. Don't take too long either: item-crushing monsters are a thing that exists, I've failed those quests due to alien slugs walking over the relic in the past. And if the relic isn't there, go to a new dungeon level and quaff the next potion.
- Some of these levels fiercely level up their monsters. If that is the case, the monsters' levels might be up to twice the nominal depth, so e.g. if you're on dungeon level 10, you'll see monsters up to level 20. These can be very dangerous. And the loot on the level won't level with the monsters, so it's often not even useful to explore. If your deity does have that kind of monster leveling factor for their temples, you should try your best to spend as little time in there as possible.
- If you completed the temple quest, you can't get the next one (there's five of them total) while still inside the temple. Since you want to get those quests as early as possible due to the quest difficulty being proportional to your experience level at the time you got each quest, *avoid* leveling up in the temple if at all possible: after completing the quest, if you're close to reaching a new level, get the hell out of there and level up somewhere else! That way you'll have a chance of receiving the next quest from that level up.
- The reward for completing such a quest is the equivalent of spending five skill points into Prayer, but without actually using up skill points. This means that the benefit scales with your prayer skill multiplier, so if that is only 0.3 you'll merely get 1.5 skill levels but a multiplier of 1.5 equals 7.5 skill levels. Or, in other words, increase your prayer skill multiplier before you do those quests to maximize the effect. This is why I almost always pick prayer skill if Fumblefingers offers it: to ensure that if and when I complete the relic quests, the multiplier is high enough to give me some actually tangible benefit.

*** Fumblefingers and princess quests ***

You did pick 980 quests when you started your char, right? Great, so you'll have those quests. If not, you only have yourself to blame; your character will end up much weaker in the end without them. So if you've read up to here and realized that your char doesn't have random quests, restart your char now and make sure faulty options like "persistent levels" are off, and then just hit Enter when the game asks how many quests you want: 980 is the default value and you just need to confirm it.

Anyway, there's two quests. Fumblefingers is the good quest and princesses are less useful but since every level has a 50/50 chance of each, you'll have roughly 490 fumblefingers and 490 princesses strewn over the dungeons. Every depth from 1 to 980 has a quest, and it doesn't matter which particular dungeon you go to as long as it's a depth that has a noncompleted quest. So if that quest on level 30 of the Land of Rhun for ancient red dragons seems too tough, you can go to level 30 of Amon Sul instead and will have the same quest there; sometimes, the dungeon's layout can make it harder or easier to complete the quests.

Why do you absolutely want to complete these quests? Simple: because they can give permanent skill boosts, including unlocking restricted skills or boosting the multiplier of a skill you already have. Sure, you can improve your skills by spending hard skill points, but that boosts them by whatever their current multiplier is and *doesn't retroactively give more of a boost if you then improve the multiplier via fumblefingers*. So it can be a good idea to hold off on your skill points, hope that some FF quests give the skill in question, and then once the multiplier is high enough, spend your points to reach higher values while spending less skill points. This is important because skill points are a limited quantity, you only get so many and then that's it so by minmaxing you can increase the amount of skills you can max out.

Don't believe me? Then let's do the math. Assume we're an archer or something, and want to increase prayer. The skill shows up as something like this:
Prayer 0.000 [0.500]
If you spend ten skill points:
Prayer 5.000 [0.500]
you'll have five skill levels. But let's assume that you obtained a couple Fumblefingers boosts, then the skill might (even if you haven't actually put any points into it) look like this:
Prayer 15.250 [0.825]
Spending ten skill points gives much more of a boost now, taking you to:
Prayer 23.500 [0.825]

So in the first case, you spend 10 skill points and gain 5 skill levels. Second case you also spend 10 skill points but gained 8 skill levels. There are a total of 294 skill points from leveling up, and every princess quest grants one more, but they will run out eventually and the more skills you can max out with those skill points, the better your char will be. Therefore, spending points on a skill with a multiplier below 0.5 is almost certainly a waste, and even a 0.5 multiplier means you spend a hundred points to reach skill level 50; a multiplier of 1.0 is much better because then reaching skill level 50 only takes fifty points. If your char starts out with a high multiplier in a given skill, you might as well pump up the skill (especially if it's part of your main build for that char, e.g. weaponmastery for a melee char or archery for an archer) right away, but if your archer started with only a 0.5 multiplier in weaponmastery it might be better to wait until you can increase that to 0.7 or so before you start putting points in it.

A neat thing about SX is that you can take skills beyond the cap of 54 via Fumblefingers. Your skill points can only increase a skill up to 4 + your experience level, and since the cap is 50 you can't boost a skill beyond 54 normally but if the skill is already at 54 and you then choose it as your FF reward, this happens:
Weaponmastery 54.000 [0.900]
->
Weaponmastery 57.700 [0.950]
All skills are coded to keep getting better with higher skill level, so this can be a great way of further boosting your character. In fact, prayer skill rewards from relic quests work like that too; I've played a priestess with 80 or so prayer skill once. Of course, if the skill is something like antimagic or sorcery you have to watch out for the downsides, which also scale with skill level :-P

*** Errata ***

As I mentioned before, this guide is inspired by Lord Dimwit's strategy guide for vanilla ToME. That one is generally very well-written, but there's two instances where his guide is needlessly vague. So I felt like addressing them here.

In "8.4 Hacking Your Way Through":
Again, how you fight Melkor is up to you; close-range, at a distance, whatever. But I should warn you that one of the tactics I've advised you to use heavily above will not work at ALL on the Void's last level. So be careful.
The thing he refers to is genocide; that last level is maliciously flagged as no-genocide. IMHO, not telling the reader of a strategy guide that this is the case is almost as malicious as the feature itself: there is *no* in-game indication that would hint at this restriction, and what's worse, vanilla ToME doesn't even give a message when you try to genocide monsters on a no-genocide level, it just fails for no apparent reason. Anyway, seriously: if I'm reading a strategy guide, I'd like to get some actual strategy, not a guessing game :-P

In "9.1 Warrior":
An absolutely stellar skill for a warrior is Mimicry at level 35; at this level you get the Arms Mimicry ability. Mimic yourself an extra set of arms and you can use a two-handed weapon with a shield and no penalty, as well as wielding a second one-handed weapon for additional benefits (make sure it's a one-handed weapon, and that it's one you have a mastery in, or bad things happen)
Not sure why we aren't told what those "bad things" are; I'm fairly certain it means that unless that weapon uses the same skill as your primary one, the masteries don't work at all. Which is stupid game design but that's how this game works; even if you have both sword- and axe-mastery, dual-wielding a sword and an axe will result in you getting no bonuses from those masteries at all, so you have to dual-wield two swords or two axes. Anyway, this is a strategy guide, right? Why leave the reader guessing at those bad effects instead of simply telling them directly so they can make an educated decision about whether they really want to dual-wield Ringil with Limbslicer?

Apart from that, Lord Dimwit's guide is very good; the reason why I made my own guide was not to criticize him or anything, but rather, it's because of the many differences between vanilla ToME and SX. Many things in his guide just don't apply cleanly to SX due to all those changes. So if you're playing SX, please don't try to follow Lord Dimwit's guide to the letter as that one is meant for vanilla ;)
